Introduction

Audio and video content uses sight, sound, motion, and emotion to engage audiences. Crafting compelling audiovisual content relies on visual context, clear dialogue, sound effects, and music working together to tell a complete story and set the desired tone In video editing and production, sound is generally divided into three distinct layers that must be balanced correctly:Dialogue: Provides clarity and information. This should always be the cleanest, clearest layer, especially for interviews.Sound Effects (SFX): Pulls the viewer into the environment (e.g., rainfall, passing cars). They can become the most important element during action sequences.Music: Acts as the glue for the video. It sets the emotional tone of the scene and helps the audience share the mood of the characters This page addresses accessibility considerations when planning, scripting, storyboarding, recording, and producing audio and video. Some of the guidance below is related to requirements in Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) and has links to a separate resource. (The Planning page of this resource introduces the WCAG Standard.) Other guidance is good practice. Additional guidance is in the resource Making Events Accessible - Checklist for meetings, conferences, training, and presentations that are remote/virtual, in-person, or hybrid: Preparing Slides and Projected MaterialDuring the Presentation For some types of videos, such as dramas, the description of the visual information cannot be smoothly handled by the speakers in the main video. For those videos, the description will be separate. Where the description is fairly short, plan space in the audio to add the description. Where the description is longer than you want to leave space in the main audio, you can record extra time in the scene to accommodate the description without having to pause the scene. That is, the same scene is shorter in the main video. In the described version, that same scene is a little longer at the beginning or the end of it.

Audio & Video

Audio and video content uses sight, sound, motion, and emotion to engage audiences. Crafting compelling audiovisual content relies on visual context, clear dialogue, sound effects, and music working together to tell a complete story and set the desired tone.Here is a breakdown of the essential components and considerations for building effective audio and video content

1. Core Audio Elements

In video editing and production, sound is generally divided into three distinct layers that must be balanced correctly: Dialogue: Provides clarity and information. This should always be the cleanest, clearest layer, especially for interviews.Sound Effects (SFX): Pulls the viewer into the environment (e.g., rainfall, passing cars). They can become the most important element during action sequences. Music: Acts as the glue for the video. It sets the emotional tone of the scene and helps the audience share the mood of the characters Great audiovisual content is accessible to all viewers, including those who are visually or hearing impaired:Audio Descriptions: Adding narration that fills in crucial visual gaps for the blind and visually impaired. Captions & Transcripts: Providing text-based redundancy for sensory characteristics so the story makes sense even if the audio is muted.

2. Core Video Elements

Visuals create immediacy, clarity, and a lasting impression. High-quality video content requires:Storyboarding & Hook: Capturing attention within the first 3–7 seconds using a strong headline, visual proof, or compelling imagery.Visual Context: Ensuring that visual overlays, text, and main subjects are well-lit, clearly visible, and properly contrasted.Motion & Pacing: Using movement and cutting techniques to keep the viewer visually engaged without inducing motion sickness or seizures. Proper Levels: Ensure that dialogue, music, and sound effects are leveled and mixed properly using audio editing meters so they sound good across all devices.Multimodal Fusion: Advanced media strategies use context-aware models that fuse visual, position, and audio features (such as background narration) together for richer and more comprehensive content.For a breakdown of how dialogue, music, and sound effects are layered to make a scene come to life:
